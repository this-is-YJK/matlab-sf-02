1. Coordinate Systems

This simulator uses two primary coordinate reference systems for defining platform positions and trajectories. Understanding the distinction between these systems is essential for correct scenario configuration.

1.1 Earth-Centered Earth-Fixed (ECEF)

The ECEF coordinate system is a Cartesian coordinate system with its origin at the Earth's center of mass. The axes are defined as:

  • X-axis: Points from the center toward the intersection of the equator (0 deg latitude) and the prime meridian (0 deg longitude)
  • Y-axis: Points from the center toward the intersection of the equator and 90 deg East longitude
  • Z-axis: Points from the center toward the North Pole

Position vector in ECEF:

$$ \vec{r}_{ECEF} = \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{bmatrix} \quad \text{(units: meters)} $$
Note: The ECEF frame rotates with the Earth, so positions expressed in ECEF are fixed relative to the Earth's surface (ignoring tectonic motion). This makes it ideal for tracking stationary or moving objects on or near Earth.

1.2 Geodetic Coordinates (LLA)

Geodetic coordinates describe a position using latitude, longitude, and altitude above a reference ellipsoid (WGS84 in this implementation).

  • Latitude (Lat): Angular distance north (+) or south (-) of the equator, ranging from -90 deg to +90 deg
  • Longitude (Lon): Angular distance east (+) or west (-) of the prime meridian, ranging from -180 deg to +180 deg
  • Altitude (Alt): Height above the reference ellipsoid surface (not mean sea level)

Geodetic position:

$$ \vec{r}_{LLA} = \begin{bmatrix} \phi \\ \lambda \\ h \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \text{latitude (deg)} \\ \text{longitude (deg)} \\ \text{altitude (m)} \end{bmatrix} $$
Property ECEF Geodetic (LLA)
Frame Type Cartesian Curvilinear
Units Meters Degrees, meters
Intuitive No Yes (human-readable)
Computation Simple (linear) Complex (nonlinear)
Use Case Simulations, tracking Navigation, waypoints

2. Coordinate Transformations

The simulator converts between geodetic (LLA) and ECEF coordinates using the WGS84 reference ellipsoid parameters.

2.1 WGS84 Ellipsoid Parameters

$$ a = 6378.137 \text{ km} \quad \text{(semi-major axis)} $$ $$ f = \frac{1}{298.257223563} \quad \text{(flattening)} $$ $$ b = a(1-f) = 6356.752 \text{ km} \quad \text{(semi-minor axis)} $$ $$ e^2 = 1 - \frac{b^2}{a^2} = 0.00669438 \quad \text{(eccentricity squared)} $$

2.2 LLA to ECEF Conversion

Given geodetic coordinates $(\phi, \lambda, h)$, the ECEF coordinates $(x, y, z)$ are computed as:

Radius of curvature in the prime vertical:

$$ N(\phi) = \frac{a}{\sqrt{1 - e^2 \sin^2 \phi}} $$

ECEF coordinates:

$$ x = (N(\phi) + h) \cos \phi \cos \lambda $$ $$ y = (N(\phi) + h) \cos \phi \sin \lambda $$ $$ z = \left( N(\phi)(1 - e^2) + h \right) \sin \phi $$
Example: A position at latitude 45 deg, longitude 90 deg, altitude 10,000 m: $$ N(45\deg) = \frac{6378137}{\sqrt{1 - 0.006694 \times \sin^2(45\deg)}} \approx 6388838 \text{ m} $$ $$ x \approx 0.638 \times 10^7 \text{ m}, \quad y \approx 0.638 \times 10^7 \text{ m}, \quad z \approx 0.450 \times 10^7 \text{ m} $$

2.3 ECEF to LLA Conversion

The inverse transformation requires an iterative solution because latitude appears on both sides of the equation. The algorithm uses:

Step 1: Calculate longitude (closed-form)

$$ \lambda = \arctan\left( \frac{y}{x} \right) $$

Step 2: Iterate for latitude

$$ \phi_{n+1} = \arctan\left( \frac{z + e^2 N(\phi_n) \sin \phi_n}{\sqrt{x^2 + y^2}} \right) $$

Step 3: Calculate altitude

$$ h = \frac{\sqrt{x^2 + y^2}}{\cos \phi} - N(\phi) $$
Implementation Note: MATLAB's geodetic2ecef and ecef2geodetic functions handle these calculations efficiently. The iteration typically converges within 3-5 steps to sub-millimeter accuracy.

3. Attitude Calculation

Aircraft attitude is described by three Euler angles: heading (yaw), pitch, and roll. The simulator derives these from the velocity vector.

3.1 Heading (Yaw) Angle

Heading is the horizontal direction of motion, measured clockwise from true North.

Given ECEF velocity components $(v_x, v_y, v_z)$:

$$ \psi = \arctan\left( \frac{v_x}{v_y} \right) \times \frac{180}{\pi} $$

Normalization to [0, 360 deg):

$$ \psi_{normalized} = \begin{cases} \psi + 360 & \text{if } \psi < 0 \\ \psi & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} $$

Where:

  • $v_x$ = velocity component toward 0 deg East longitude
  • $v_y$ = velocity component toward the North Pole (at origin)

3.2 Pitch Angle

Pitch is the angle between the velocity vector and the local horizontal plane.

Horizontal speed:

$$ v_{horiz} = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2} $$

Total speed:

$$ v_{total} = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2 + v_z^2} $$

Pitch angle:

$$ \theta = \arcsin\left( \frac{v_z}{v_{total}} \right) \times \frac{180}{\pi} $$
Physical interpretation:
  • $\theta > 0$: Climbing (velocity vector above horizontal)
  • $\theta = 0$: Level flight
  • $\theta < 0$: Descending (velocity vector below horizontal)

3.3 Roll Angle

Roll (bank angle) is estimated from the rate of heading change, based on the coordinated turn assumption.

Heading rate from successive measurements:

$$ \dot{\psi} = \frac{\psi_{current} - \psi_{previous}}{\Delta t} $$

Coordinated turn equation:

$$ \tan(\phi_{roll}) = \frac{v_{horiz} \cdot \dot{\psi}}{g} $$

Roll angle:

$$ \phi_{roll} = \arctan\left( \frac{v_{horiz} \cdot \dot{\psi}}{g} \right) \times \frac{180}{\pi} $$

Where $g = 9.81$ m/s$^2$ is the acceleration due to gravity.

Coordinated Turn Assumption: In a coordinated turn, the aircraft banks such that the lift force provides both the vertical component (to counteract weight) and the horizontal component (to provide centripetal acceleration for the turn). Zero sideslip is maintained.

3.4 Attitude Smoothing

To prevent unrealistic jumps in attitude angles (which would imply infinite angular acceleration), the simulator applies rate limiting:

$$ \psi_{output} = \begin{cases} \psi_{prev} + \dot{\psi}_{max} \cdot \Delta t & \text{if } |\Delta\psi| > \dot{\psi}_{max} \cdot \Delta t \\ \psi_{current} & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} $$

Default maximum turn rate: 3 deg/s for fixed-wing aircraft.

4. Trajectory Generation

Platform trajectories are generated using MATLAB's waypointTrajectory, which interpolates between user-defined waypoints.

4.1 Waypoint Interpolation

Given waypoints $\vec{r}_1, \vec{r}_2, \ldots, \vec{r}_n$ at corresponding times $t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_n$, the trajectory is computed as:

Position interpolation along segment $i$ (from $\vec{r}_i$ to $\vec{r}_{i+1}$):

$$ \vec{r}(t) = \vec{r}_i + \frac{t - t_i}{t_{i+1} - t_i} (\vec{r}_{i+1} - \vec{r}_i) $$

The position varies linearly, resulting in constant velocity between waypoints. Velocity magnitude is determined by the distance and time between consecutive waypoints:

$$ \vec{v}_i = \frac{\vec{r}_{i+1} - \vec{r}_i}{t_{i+1} - t_i} $$

4.2 Smoothed Trajectory

MATLAB's waypointTrajectory provides several interpolation methods. By default, it uses cubic polynomial interpolation for smoother velocity transitions:

Cubic Hermite spline:

$$ \vec{r}(t) = h_{00}(s) \vec{r}_i + h_{10}(s) (t_{i+1}-t_i) \vec{v}_i \cdot 0 + h_{01}(s) \vec{r}_{i+1} + h_{11}(s) (t_{i+1}-t_i) \vec{v}_{i+1} \cdot 0 $$

where $s = \frac{t - t_i}{t_{i+1} - t_i}$ is the normalized parameter and $h_{00}, h_{10}, h_{01}, h_{11}$ are the Hermite basis functions.

4.3 Limitations

The linear waypoint model has inherent limitations for realistic aircraft simulation:

Limitation Impact Mitigation
Instant velocity change at waypoints Unrealistic accelerations Add intermediate waypoints
No turn rate limiting Unrealistic turns Post-process attitude
No climb rate limiting Unrealistic climbs Add climb/descent waypoints
Ignored terrain Ground collisions Manual altitude planning
Recommended practice: For realistic aircraft trajectories, include intermediate waypoints at turn points with a straight segment before and after the turn. This creates a path: straight → gradual turn → straight.

5. WGS84 Reference Ellipsoid

The World Geodetic System 1984 (WGS84) is the standard reference ellipsoid used by GPS and this simulator.

5.1 Physical Definition

Ellipsoid surface equation:

$$ \frac{x^2 + y^2}{a^2} + \frac{z^2}{b^2} = 1 $$

where:

$$ a = 6,378,137 \text{ m (equatorial radius)} $$ $$ b = 6,356,752.314 \text{ m (polar radius)} $$

5.2 Key Parameters

Parameter Symbol Value Definition
Semi-major axis $a$ 6,378,137 m Equatorial radius
Semi-minor axis $b$ 6,356,752 m Polar radius
Flattening $f$ 1/298.257 $(a-b)/a$
First eccentricity squared $e^2$ 0.00669438 $(a^2-b^2)/a^2$
Angular velocity $\omega$ 7.292115e-5 rad/s Earth's rotation rate
Gravitational constant $GM$ 3.986004e14 m$^3$/s$^2$ Earth's mass times $G$

5.3 Height Reference

Important: WGS84 altitude ($h$) is height above the ellipsoid surface, not above mean sea level (MSL).

$$ h_{WGS84} = h_{MSL} + N $$

Where $N$ is the geoid height (undulation), which ranges from about -85 m to +85 m globally.

For this simulator: We use WGS84 ellipsoid height directly. In real-world applications, this can introduce errors up to ~50 m in extreme cases. For tactical simulations, this is generally acceptable. For precision applications, use EGM2008 geoid corrections.

6. Sensor Models

The simulator implements three sensor types, each with distinct measurement models and detection physics.

6.1 Radar Sensor Model

Active radar provides range, azimuth, elevation, and range-rate measurements from reflected RF energy.

Spherical coordinate conversion:

$$ r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2} $$ $$ \alpha = \arctan\left(\frac{y}{x}\right) \times \frac{180}{\pi} $$ $$ \epsilon = \arctan\left(\frac{z}{\sqrt{x^2 + y^2}}\right) \times \frac{180}{\pi} $$

Measurement model (with noise):

$$ r_{meas} = r_{true} + \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_r) $$ $$ \alpha_{meas} = \alpha_{true} + \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_\alpha) $$ $$ \epsilon_{meas} = \epsilon_{true} + \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_\epsilon) $$ $$ \dot{r}_{meas} = \dot{r}_{true} + \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_{\dot{r}}) $$

6.2 Signal-to-Noise Ratio

Radar detection depends on SNR, which decreases with the fourth power of range.

Radar equation (simplified):

$$ P_r = \frac{P_t G^2 \sigma \lambda^2}{(4\pi)^3 R^4} $$

SNR model:

$$ SNR_{dB} = SNR_{ref} - 40 \log_{10}\left(\frac{R}{R_{ref}}\right) $$

where $SNR_{ref}$ is the reference SNR at range $R_{ref}$.

6.3 Probability of Detection

Detection probability follows a smooth threshold model based on SNR.

$$ P_d = \begin{cases} 0.01 & \text{if } SNR < 5 \text{ dB} \\ 0.99 & \text{if } SNR > 25 \text{ dB} \\ 0.5 + 0.5 \tanh\left(\frac{SNR - SNR_{th}}{4}\right) & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} $$

where $SNR_{th} \approx 13$ dB is the detection threshold.

6.4 ESM (Radar Warning Receiver)

ESM is a passive sensor detecting radar emissions. Provides angle-of-arrival only.

Angle-only measurements:

$$ \alpha_{meas} = \alpha_{true} + \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_\alpha^{ESM}) $$ $$ \epsilon_{meas} = \epsilon_{true} + \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_\epsilon^{ESM}) $$

Signal strength (decreases with $R^2$):

$$ S = S_{ref} \left(\frac{R_{ref}}{R}\right)^2 $$
ESM Detection: Targets must have active radar emissions to be detected by ESM. Targets without emitters are invisible.

6.5 IR Sensor Model

Infrared sensors detect thermal signatures passively. Detection depends on target temperature and atmospheric conditions.

IR intensity (inverse square + attenuation):

$$ I = I_{base} \left(\frac{R_{ref}}{R}\right)^2 \cdot 10^{-\alpha R / 10} $$

where $\alpha$ is atmospheric attenuation (typically ~0.5 dB/km at low altitude).

IR signature by target type:

Fighter (afterburning)$I_{base} = 100$
Bomber$I_{base} = 80$
Missile (rocket)$I_{base} = 200$
Transport$I_{base} = 40$

7. Sensor Geometry and Mounting

7.1 Sensor-to-World Transformation

Sensor measurements are in the sensor body frame. The full transformation chain is:

$$ \vec{r}_{world} = T_{sensor \rightarrow world} \cdot T_{mount} \cdot \vec{r}_{sensor} $$

Where:

$$ T_{sensor \rightarrow world} = T_{body \rightarrow world} \cdot T_{mount} $$

Platform body to world:

$$ T_{body \rightarrow world} = R_z(\psi) R_y(\theta) R_x(\phi) $$

using 3-2-1 Euler angles (yaw-pitch-roll).

7.2 Mount Offset

Sensors can be offset from the platform centroid and canted at an angle.

Mount position offset:

$$ \vec{r}_{sensor} = \vec{r}_{platform} + R_{body} \cdot \vec{\Delta}_{mount} $$

Mount orientation offset:

$$ R_{sensor} = R_{body} \cdot R_{mount} $$
Example: Nose-mounted radar 2m below and 1m forward of centroid:
  • Mount position: $[1, 0, -2]$ m (body frame)
  • Mount orientation: $[0, 0, 0]$ deg (aligned with body)

7.3 Field of View Model

Targets are detected only if within the sensor's angular FOV.

FOV check:

$$ |\alpha_{target}| \leq \frac{\Omega_{az}}{2} \quad \text{and} \quad |\epsilon_{target}| \leq \frac{\Omega_{el}}{2} $$

where $\Omega_{az}$ and $\Omega_{el}$ are total azimuth and elevation FOV.

Sensor Azimuth FOV Elevation FOV Notes
Surveillance Radar 120 deg 40 deg Sector scan
ESM 360 deg 60 deg Full azimuth coverage
IRST 90 deg 30 deg Sector, passive

8. Measurement Error Models

Each sensor has characteristic noise parameters that model measurement uncertainty.

8.1 Gaussian Noise Model

All sensor noise is modeled as additive zero-mean Gaussian.

$$ z = h(x) + v, \quad v \sim \mathcal{N}(0, R) $$

Measurement covariance:

$$ R = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_r^2 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \sigma_\alpha^2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \sigma_\epsilon^2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \sigma_{\dot{r}}^2 \end{bmatrix} $$

Search Radar 50 0.5 0.5 10
Fire Control Radar 10 0.1 0.1 2
ESM - 2.0 3.0 -
IRST ~15%$\times$R 1.0 1.5 -
